§ 01 — Mission
The build

APSCO's Student Small Satellite Competition asks teams to design, build and demonstrate a complete CubeSat — not just an experiment on a host bus. Our group, the BD-BRACU Mavericks at BRAC University, took that as a chance to put every subsystem in the hands of student engineers.

As team lead, my work cut across mechanical, electrical and software layers — keeping the subsystem leads aligned, owning the integration plan, and standing in for whichever board was on fire that week.

§ 02 — Sub-systems
Bus architecture

EPS
Electrical Power System
Solar harvesting, battery management and regulated rails for the bus and payload — sized against the orbit power budget.
OBC
On-Board Computer
Microcontroller-based flight computer handling scheduling, telemetry, and command decoding under tight resource budgets.
COM
Communications
UHF/VHF radio link to the ground station, framed for low-data-rate beaconing and reliable long-distance reception.
ADCS
Attitude Determination & Control
Sensor fusion (IMU + sun sensor) and control law for nadir-pointing — sized for the satellite's moment of inertia.
ANT
Antenna
Deployable antenna design and integration with the structure; pattern and gain validated against the link budget.
GS
Ground Station
Tracking, doppler correction and decoded telemetry — closing the loop between the satellite and its operators.
STR
Structure
3U mechanical design in SOLIDWORKS — mass budget, thermal mounts and CubeSat-spec rail tolerances.
SIM
Orbital Simulation
Pass timing, eclipse fraction and ground-station contact windows analysed to drive duty-cycle and thermal planning.
PYL
Payload
Image-processing pipeline planned around the downlink budget — picking what to send when the link window is short.

§ 03 — What I took away
The lessons

+ SYSTEMS THINKING
A CubeSat exposes you to every constraint a real spacecraft has — power, link, thermal, mass — in miniature. Trade-offs that span subsystems become daily decisions.
+ LEADING ENGINEERS
Aligning eight subsystem leads, managing the integration schedule, and being the person who carries the integration risk taught me what technical leadership actually looks like in practice.
+ HARDWARE DISCIPLINE
PCBs ship, boards die, schedules slip — building under a competition deadline forced rigour around revision control, test fixtures and integration-day procedures.
+ COMMUNICATIONS
Ground-station design, link budgeting and the amateur-radio license that came with it have stayed useful — I still see RF design as a craft to come back to.
