APSCO CubeSat — 3U Mission

Team Lead · BD-BRACU Mavericks Jul 2023 – May 2024 APSCO Student Small Satellite Competition

A full-stack 3U CubeSat developed for the APSCO Student Small Satellite Competition — every subsystem owned in-house, from the EPS and OBC up to the ground station and orbital simulation.

Mission & Role

APSCO's Student Small Satellite Competition asks teams to design, build, and demonstrate a complete CubeSat. Our group, the BD-BRACU Mavericks at BRAC University, took that as a chance to put every subsystem in the hands of student engineers.

As team lead, my work cut across mechanical, electrical, and software layers — keeping the subsystem leads aligned, owning the integration plan, and standing in for whichever board was under pressure that week.

Bus Architecture

All nine subsystems were designed in-house:

  • EPS (Electrical Power System) — Solar harvesting, battery management, and regulated rails sized against the orbit power budget.
  • OBC (On-Board Computer) — Microcontroller-based flight computer for scheduling, telemetry, and command decoding under tight resource constraints.
  • COM (Communications) — UHF/VHF radio link to the ground station, framed for low-data-rate beaconing and reliable long-distance reception.
  • ADCS (Attitude Determination & Control) — Sensor fusion (IMU + sun sensor) and control law for nadir-pointing, sized for the satellite's moment of inertia.
  • ANT (Antenna) — Deployable antenna design and integration; pattern and gain validated against the link budget.
  • GS (Ground Station) — Tracking, Doppler correction, and decoded telemetry — closing the loop between satellite and operators.
  • STR (Structure) — 3U mechanical design in SOLIDWORKS with mass budget, thermal mounts, and CubeSat-spec rail tolerances.
  • SIM (Orbital Simulation) — Pass timing, eclipse fraction, and contact windows to drive duty-cycle and thermal planning.
  • PYL (Payload) — Image-processing pipeline planned around the downlink budget.

Lessons Taken Away

  • Systems thinking — A CubeSat exposes every constraint a real spacecraft has — power, link, thermal, mass — in miniature. Subsystem-spanning trade-offs become daily decisions.
  • Leading engineers — Aligning eight subsystem leads, managing the integration schedule, and carrying integration risk taught me what technical leadership actually looks like in practice.
  • Hardware discipline — PCBs ship, boards die, schedules slip. Building under a competition deadline forced rigour around revision control, test fixtures, and integration procedures.
  • RF communications — Ground-station design, link budgeting, and the amateur-radio licence that came with it have stayed useful well beyond the project.
